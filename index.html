Diversity in Tech

HCI Final Project Lauren Back, Jocelyn Hsu, Kritika Sharma, Alexander Vargas

Background

Diversity is a crucial aspect of the tech industry. Hiring employees from a wide range of backgrounds will allow companies to build products useful for a variety of consumers - “the greater the representation, the higher the likelihood of outperformance” (McKinsey). Despite the advantages, the tech industry remains not nearly as diverse as its users. Below, we highlight the main categories of disparity: gender, ethnicity, socioeconomic background, and education.

Gender

While women make up 47% of the employed U.S. population, only 25% of positions in tech are held by women. The most common path to tech is through a STEM degree. As of 2020, women make up 45% of those who received a STEM degree, but within STEM, only 16% and 21% of computer science and engineering degree recipients, respectively, are held by women. In the workplace, females also receive 13% lower salaries than their male counterparts. Alarmingly, over 50% of women have reported experiencing inequality, discrimination, and/or sexual harrassment.

Percentage of women in engineering fields over time

Despite these concerning statistics, over the past decade, the percentage of women in engineering fields has been steadily decreasing. Software developers, one of the most high-demand jobs, has seen nearly a 5% decrease the the percentage of women. This is discouraging, as we've seen a plethora of companies succeed under the leadership and employement of gender-diverse companies. 23andMe, with 51:49 female to male ratio and guaranteed equal gender pay, has made incredible progress in genetic research and achieved consistently increasing profit over the past years. Likewise, Etsy, with 56% of female employees and 87% women entrepreneurs, has revolutionized the way people buy and sell handmade goods and exceeded Wall Street expectations.

Given these trends, it’s clear that gender disparity occurs at all parts of the journey to become a contributor in tech and is worsening over the years. At the hiring level, inequalities should be addressed to support gender diversity.

Root cause of gender bias: Education

Overall, women are doing better than ever, where education is concerned. According to recent trends, women are more likely than men to be enrolled in college, with nearly 67% of all college students identifying as female. Women are also more likely to earn diplomas, with college completion rates now reaching 65%!

However, these on-the-rise figures don’t reach many tech fields, and some numbers seem to spiral backward. In the mid-1980s, 37% of computer science majors were women; now, it’s dipped to 16%.

According to the 2018-2019 report from the National Center of Educational Statistics, women make up only 21.31% of engineering graduates with a bachelor’s, 28.44% with a master’s, and 24.39% graduate with a doctorate. The fields of study we’ve considered are computer and information sciences, Engineering and engineering technologies, and a few interdisciplinary studies like HCI and Systems Engineering.

In addition, a Google survey reported that the perpetuated influence of this retrograde thinking and biases has significantly impacted future generations of young women in tech, some of whom now believe that computer science is not essential.

Ethnicity

The chart illustrates the ethnic breakdown for some of the top tech companies today, with the default graph showing the breakdown for white employees. The first bar shows the population of that race/ethnicity in the U.S., providing a point of reference.

The data showed that Asians make up about 29% of tech employees, while only constituting 6% of the US population. Latinos make up 18% of the U.S. population, but only around 7% of tech employees. Similarly, Black people make up 13% of the population, but only 5% of tech employees. This pattern persists and becomes even greater in higher, leadership roles within companies. It is clear Latino and Black people are under-represented minorities in tech.

There are several problems that contribute to this discrepancy, including unequal access to resources and implicit bias in the hiring process. There have been numerous studies which show that employers are much more likely to call back applicants with “white” names than an applicant with the same qualifications/resume but with “black” names. While more attention has been placed on diversity and inclusion, there still remains a large gap for improvement.

Its x axis is divided into fifths, with the leftmost being the lowest fifth and the rightmost being the lowest fifth.

The statistics shows that there is a noticeable increase and disparity in unemployment and lack of enrollment in further education as you go towards the lowest fifth, specifically going from 5 to 26 for both. This shows that those who have more opportunities from their socioeconomic background do have an advantage and greater likelihood of entering the tech field.

Takeaways

What can the tech industry do now to close the gender gap?

Amplify Minority's Voices

In tech—as in all other workplaces—we need to amplify aggressively. Listen to minorities, actively include them in conversations and give credit where it’s (desperately) due. The company’s business and the quality of the work environment will only improve.

Ditch the Damaging Dialogue

The seemingly-harmless remarks are anything but benign. They’re not merely worn-out phrases; they’re affronts perpetuating harmful stereotypes and fostering an atmosphere of prejudice. After all, it’s one of the significant issues: 39% of women say that gender bias in the workplace is why they’re underrepresented in technology.

Acknowledge Faults and Address Them

Google is an excellent example of this. The tech giant is no stranger to the gender gap. Its workforce is only 33% female, and nearly 72% of its leadership is male. And in 2017, the company faced lawsuits from former female employees who alleged the tech giant enacted systemic pay discrimination and the denial of promotions and other growth opportunities. However, since the release of these statistics and the litigation news, Google has made strides to improve its numbers and work culture. In 2014, it announced a $50 million investment in programs that would help spark girls’ interest in STEM education, including a “Made With Code” campaign and partnerships with Girls Who Code, Black Girls Code, and the Girl Scouts of America.
